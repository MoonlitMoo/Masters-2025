\chapter[Analysis Tools]{Analysis Methods}
\label{Chap:Analysis}

The primary goal of this thesis is to extract reliable flux density measurements of the minihalo emisssion. These values are then able to be compared with previous works to estimate the spectral index of the emission. With the calibrated datasets, several further steps in the image domain are necessary to isolate the diffuse emission from point sources, define the spatial region of the emission, and estimate the associated uncertainties. This chapter details the methods used to reach a total flux measurement for the minihalo, with the following chapter presenting the results per cluster. 

\section{Removal of contaminating sources}
\label{sec:source_subtraction}
Contamination of the minihalo emission can occur in several ways, such as AGN embedded within the spatial region of the minihalo and positive or negative radial artefacts from the convolution of the PSF with poorly modelled sources. These bias the total minihalo flux, necessitating the removal of contaminating sources. This was achieved by forming a sky model of the emission and using the Fourier transformed model to subtract from the observed visibilities. The resulting source-subtracted images then depict the minihalo emission without contamination. Unstable sources were primarily attributed to time-variability between observation dates, resulting in an unphysical model and imaging artefacts. Independent models of the source were created for each dataset and subtracted individually, so that the joint image no longer contained the variable source. An accurate model of the source is necessary for subtraction, since if the model contains minihalo emission, the resulting image will feature a negative bowl where it was removed. Conversely, if the model underestimates the flux, the minihalo flux will be artificially peaked and the total flux density will be boosted. 

To ensure that the subtraction model does not include diffuse emission, it is desirable to remove flux corresponding to large-scale emission. Since baseline length corresponds to spatial scale in the image, removing short baselines will cause the large scale minihalo emission to be strongly suppressed in the image. This was achieved by specifying a \texttt{uvrange} in \texttt{tclean}, which allows specification of a baseline cutoff. To determine an optimal uv value to perform the cutoff, testing was performed on the observations of RXJ1720+2638. The spatial region of this minihalo is contaminated with the bright BCG, while a variable AGN to the south west caused negative radial spikes that in proximity to the minihalo emission (see Figure \ref{fig:contaminating_emission}).

To evaluate an appropriate cutoff, the lower uv bound was varied and a Gaussian point source was fitted to the BCG using the \texttt{imfit} task for each dataset. As this region contains the sum of the minihalo and BCG fluxes, there exists a cutoff value for which the BCG emission alone remains, and the peak flux will converge to a constant value. The peak flux was used instead of the integrated flux as the BCG represents an unresolved point source, though these values will also converge as extended emission is removed. Figure \ref{fig:uvrange_cutoff} shows the results of the testing, with convergence at baselines $>10$ k$\lambda$. The peak values for the D-configuration datasets are inconsistent at low cutoffs due to the greater minihalo contamination (from higher brightness sensitivity). This caused the fitted Gaussian to exhibit a low peak and wide spread, boosting the integrated flux over the extended region. A minimum cutoff was chosen at 12 k$\lambda$ to ensure no minihalo remained, while minimising the effect on the signal-to-noise ratio. 

A clear difference of approximately 10\% in the measured AGN flux exists between the D-configuration observations. This may be due to calibration error or true variability. This important distinction is discussed in more detail in Section \ref{sec:calibration_error}. The models for AGN subtraction used a 12 k$\lambda$ minimum uv cut, and all models were subtracted on an individual dataset basis to prevent under- or over-subtraction due to time-variability.

\section{Minihalo spatial extent}
To estimate the total minihalo flux density, the individual fluxes within the spatial extent of the minihalo must be integrated. For valid comparisons with previous studies, the ideal approach would use an identical spatial mask. However, these masks are often not available or lack detailed information. For these clusters, the spatial extent is instead estimated as the sum of pixels with flux $>3\sigma$, where the noise $\sigma$ was determined from the rms per beam across the residual image. The initial binary selection mask was segmented into separate emission structures. The minihalo region was identified and confirmed to be isolated from other discrete sources prior to final summation. 

One exception was RXJ1720+2638, as \citet{Giacintucci2014a} defined a mask used for calculation of the minihalo emission (see Figure 2b). Furthermore, they categorise a central and tail region. This allowed the reconstruction of the mask, which was used in results of this thesis. 

\section{Flux density uncertainty}
\label{sec:calibration_error}
There are three primary sources of error for estimations in the total flux density of minihalo emission, due to AGN subtraction $\sigma_{AGN}$, spatial image noise $\sigma_{noise}$, and calibration error $\sigma_{cal}$. The total uncertainty is then the rms of the components,\begin{equation}
    \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma_{AGN}^2 + \sigma_{noise}^2 + \sigma_{cal}^2} 
\end{equation}

Secondary calibrators and compact AGN used for amplitude and phase calibration can show flux density fluctuations between 5--10\% at month- to year-timescales \citep{Kurinsky2013}. This is in addition to the 3\% intrinsic uncertainty at 8--12 GHz in the primary calibrators \cite{Perley2017}. Any variation in a target source is therefore a combination of calibrator variability and residual calibration errors. This motivates the inclusion of a systematic calibration uncertainty to account for fluctuations in the primary and secondary calibrators. To estimate this effect, the modelled power-law spectra of the secondary calibrators were compared between consecutive month observations to constrain residual errors. Such month-to-month variations should be minimal, broadband, and comparable to the intrinsic primary calibrator uncertainty. In contrast, highly variable sources are expected to exhibit changes in the spectral shape in addition to large amplitude variations. A purely broadband change can be modelled as $S_{obs1} = C\>S_{obs2}$, where $C$ is a constant scaling factor, and was fit using $\chi^2$ minimisation. Deviations from the linear relation indicate changes in the spectral shape, resulting in large residuals and leading to the exclusion of such cases from further analysis. The error-weighted absolute variation over all clusters is measured at 2.14$\%$, with only a single cluster exceeding 5\% deviation (Figure \ref{fig:secondary_variation}). This level of variation is consistent with the expected time variability of calibration sources, and all larger deviations correspond to changes in spectral shape. The calibration error is therefore conservatively constrained to be 5\%.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Chapter4/cluster_epoch_plots/secondary_variation.pdf}
    \caption{Month-to-month broadband variation for all secondary calibrators. The year of measured variation is denoted by colour. A good fit (circles) corresponds to highly linear broadband scaling, while a poor fit (squares) indicates changes in spectral shape. The adopted calibration uncertainty (5\%) is marked for comparison.}
    \label{fig:secondary_variation}
\end{figure}

The error from the subtraction of AGN can be attributed to errors in the imaging. This corresponds to the residual rms per beam during AGN modelling $\sigma_{rms, AGN}$, weighted by the number of beams covering the AGN. Since these are compact sources, there is only a single beam, and the error is simply $\sigma_{AGN} = \sigma_{rms, AGN}$. This differs to the estimate used by \citet{Giacintucci2014a}, where $\sigma_{AGN}^2 = (A_{AGN} \times I_{MH})^2$ with $A_{AGN}$ the area of the radio galaxy and $I_{MH}$ the average surface brightness of the minihalo near the source. This makes the conservative assumption that the flux of the minihalo is entirely erroneous at the location of the subtraction. For a clean subtraction, the former method is more realistic, and was used. The measured flux density for any notable sources are reported in the results. For highly variable sources exhibiting significant differences between observations, the flux is reported as the rms-weighted mean with the associated uncertainty taken as the maximum of the propagated error and the statistical scatter between the observations.

The image noise from the spatial sum over the minihalo region is calculated as the residual rms per beam of the minihalo image, weighted by the beams in spatial region. The number of beams is given by $N_{beam} = A_{MH} / A_{beam}$, where $A_{MH}$ is the area of the minihalo  and $A_{beam}$ is the restoring beam area. This gives the total image noise as $\sigma_{noise}^2 = (\sigma_{rms}\sqrt{N_beam})^2$.
