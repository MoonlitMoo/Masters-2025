\section{RXJ1720+2638}
\label{sec:rxj1720+2638}
RXJ1720+2638 (also known as RXJ1720.1+2638) is a cool core galaxy cluster at $z = 0.160$ \citep{Owers2011}. Early X-ray observations detected the presence of two cold fronts, a sharp edge to the southeast and an extended plateau to the northwest \citep{Mazzotta2001}. This suggested the presence of a dense, cool gas core ($T \approx 4$ keV) embedded within a more diffuse and hotter ICM ($T\approx10$ keV), with the cold fronts likely arising via a minor merger. Weak gravitational lensing studies \citep{Okabe2010}, together with galaxy density mapping \citep{Owers2011}, revealed the presence of a substructure approximately 550 kpc north of the cluster core. \citet{Owers2011} suggest that a northward passage of this substructure, passing east of the cluster core, provided the perturbation required to induce large-scale gas sloshing. RXJ1720+2638 was one of the first two clusters where cold fronts and minihalos were found to exhibit a spatial correlation, with the minihalo emission bounded by the cold fronts \citep{Mazotta2008}. 

A detailed spectral study was performed by \citet{Giacintucci2014a}, which analysed observations at six frequencies from 317 MHz to 8.4 GHz taken by the GMRT and VLA. To perform accurate comparisons between these frequencies, the baseline range was limited to 1--50 k$\lambda$, to enforce a unified sampling of the uv plane. They noted three primary radio components in the core region, the central radio galaxy (BCG, $\alpha = 0.87\pm0.03$), a head-tail radio galaxy to the northeast with the tail to the south ($\alpha = 1.05\pm0.04$, steepening down the tail), and diffuse minihalo emission. The BCG was noted to be unresolved with no jets or lobes detected at scales larger than 1.4 kpc. Higher angular resolution observations by the extended Multi-Element Radio Linked Interferometer Network (e-MERLIN) at 4.76 GHz found the BCG to exhibit sub-kpc jets \citep{Perrott2023}. These jets extend approximately 0.8 kpc to both the east and west, although though uncertainties in the recovered flux suggest an additional undetected extent. \citet{Giacintucci2014a} separated the emission into a central region with a diameter of 160 kpc and a tail extending approximately 200 kpc to the south. These were found to have spectral indices of $\alpha_c = 1.0\pm0.1$ and $\alpha_t = 1.5\pm0.1$, respectively. The core region exhibits a relatively flat spectral index across its extent, while clear spectral steepening is observed along the tail. Low-frequency LOFAR images at 144 MHz support the flat spectral index in the core, with pronounced steepening across the cold fronts \citet{Savini2019}. The spectral index for the core is consistent up to 4.86 GHz, however, the 8.44 GHz measurement was lower than predicted by the single power-law model. While this could indicate possible steepening, the 4.86 GHz and 8.44 GHz observations suffered from significanly poorer uv sampling, likely leading to flux losses through the filtering of large-scale emission. \citet{Perrott2021} performed a high-frequency measurement of the central minihalo region at 15.5 GHz, using the Arcminute Microkelvin Imager (AMI). By constructing a model of the minihalo directly in the uv plane to account for spatial filtering, they found that a single power-law with $\alpha = 1.0$ remains consistent up to 15.5 GHz. The 8.44 GHz data is similarly reprocessed and modelled in the uv plane, with the resulting best fitting model closely matching the single power-law spectrum. This demonstrated the previously observed steepening was caused by spatial filtering, rather than an intrinsic physical process. 

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
During imaging, two contaminating sources were identified prior to minihalo flux estimation (see Figure \ref{fig:rxj1720+2638_full_image}). These comprised the BCG (S1), embedded within the minihalo emission, and a variable AGN (S3) to the southwest. These sources were modelled individually per dataset with a uv cut of 15 k$\lambda$ and subsequently subtracted. The head-tail source (S2) was well cleaned, with its tail emission isolated from that of the minihalo. A balance of beam sensitivity and PSF suppression was obtained using a \texttt{robust} of 1. The multi-scale \texttt{tclean} scales were set to $[0, 4, 8, 16, 32]$ with a \texttt{smallscalebias} of $-0.5$. \citet{Giacintucci2014a} provide details of the masking region, including the separation of centre and tail regions. A uv constraint of 1--50 k$\lambda$ was applied to ensure matched sampling. This allowed an accurate reproduction of these regions for analysis and comparison to previous studies.

The total minihalo flux density was measured as $8.50\pm0.44$ mJy, with the majority $7.54\pm0.39$ mJy attributed to the central region and only $0.96\pm0.11$ mJy arising from the tail. The measured flux densities for relevant sources and regions are provided in Table \ref{tab:flux_densities}. The 10 GHz image is shown in Figure \ref{fig:rxj1720+2638_sub_image}. Spectral indices were fitted for the total minihalo emission, the central region, the tail, and the BCG using flux densities from this work combined with measurements from \citet{Giacintucci2014a} (excluding 8.46 GHz). The resulting spectral energy distributions are shown in Figure \ref{fig:rxj1720+2638_seds}. The spectral index of the central minihalo region was $\alpha_c = 1.08\pm0.06$, with a steeper tail region of $1.25\pm0.07$. The central results agree well with the previous measurements, while the tail found to be slightly flatter on average.

The BCG was measured to vary $\sim\!$20\% over one year (between C and D-configuration runs) and $\sim\!$10\% over one month (subsequent D-configurations). This degree of variation could suggest a systematic calibration error between the D-configuration observations. A systematic offset of $0.7$\% was observed in the flux density models of the secondary calibrator for these observations. Other point sources in the image were measured and no systematic offsets were observed. Given the lack of systematic offsets in other sources, together with the presence of active jets from the BCG, the observed variability is likely real.

\section{A478}

A478 is a nearby cool core galaxy cluster at a redshift of $z = 0.088$ \citep{Liu2024}. Chandra observations reveal a relaxed cluster morphology with a slightly elliptical ICM structure and a central BCG hosting a double-lobed AGN \citep{Sun2003, Bourdin2008}. The core temperature is $T \approx 1.54$ keV, increasing to $T \approx 7.6$ keV at a radius of 260 kpc \citep{FanLam2024}. Two small ($\sim\!10$ kpc) X-ray cavities to the northeast and southwest are spatially aligned with the lobes of the BCG, indicating interaction between the BCG and the ICM \citep{Sun2003}. A cold front is detected approximately 60 kpc to the southwest of the cluster core \citep{Markevitch2003}. 

In the initial detection of minihalo emission, \citet{Giacintucci2014} identified several nearby radio sources to the minihalo. At 1.4 GHz, these include the central BCG (S1); a head-tail to the east with a tail to the northwest (S4); a second head-tail embedded in the tail of S4 and extending to the northeast (S2); and an unresolved source (S3) to the north of S2 and S4. The latter source, S3, is likely a background source due to the absence of an optical counterpart. \citet{Giacintucci2014} find the minihalo to be slightly more extended to the northeast ($R_{max} \sim 150$ kpc) compared to the other directions ($R_{min}\sim150$ kpc). This region blends with the tail of S4, resulting in a large uncertainty in the minihalo flux estimate due to difficulty in separating the tail emission from the diffuse minihalo. They find the minihalo emission to be largely confined by the southwest cold front, with a total flux density of $16.6\pm3$ mJy. LOFAR imaging at 144 MHz does not detect the diffuse minihalo emission, placing an upper limit on the spectral index of $\alpha < 1$, implying a flatter spectrum than most observed minihalos \citep{Savini2019}. Using archival GMRT data, \citet{Savini2019} find S1 and S4 to have a spectral indices of $\sim\!1.1$ and $\sim\!0.6$, respectively, between 144 and 610 MHz. 

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
The minihalo detected at 10 GHz is significantly smaller than observed at 1.4 GHz, measuring approximately 80 kpc north-south and 45 kpc east-west. This resulted in only two contaminating sources, S1 and S4 (see Figure \ref{fig:a478_image}). The S4 AGN doubled in brightness in the month between the D-configuration observations, causing severe PSF artefacts in the joint image. As such, this source was modelled on a per-dataset basis for the final self-calibration rounds. No comparable variation was observed in other compact sources, suggesting this behaviour is consistent with an AGN flare. The BCG exhibited variability consistent with the estimated calibration uncertainty over all observations. The sources S2 and S3 are detected, but outside the radius of the observed minihalo. Three faint compact sources are located between S2 and the minihalo, but are spatially separate from the minihalo emission at the $3\sigma$ level. Point subtraction was therefore limited to S1 and S4 (including the tail), using the standard 12 k$\lambda$ uv cut. This uv cut resulted in some residual extended emission from S4, however, it was spatially distinct and the artefacts were successfully suppressed. The minihalo was modelled with scales $[0, 2, 4, 8, 16]$, \texttt{smallscalebias} of -0.5, and a \texttt{robust} of 1. The masking region was not explictly defined in \citep{Giacintucci2014}, flux estimation mask was determined using the $3\sigma$ region as discussed in the previous chapter.

The total minihalo flux was measured as $0.31 \pm 0.02$ mJy. Associated compact sources are listed in Table \ref{tab:flux_densities}. Combining this measurement with the 1.4 GHz measurement by \citet{Giacintucci2014}, the resulting spectral index is $\alpha=2.02\pm0.10$. This is exceptionally steep for a minihalo and is inconsistent with the spectral index upper limit reported by \citep{Savini2019}. To investigate further, the 8--12 GHz observation was split into four 1 GHz sub-bands. Each sub-band was imaged indepenently, and the total minihalo flux was computed using the spatial mask from the joint image. The resulting flux densities are listed in Table \ref{tab:a478_subbands}. Over the 8--12 GHz range the spectral index is even steeper at $\alpha=3.42\pm0.30$. The BCG spectral index is $0.78\pm0.02$, close to $0.88\pm0.03$ measured over the full 1.4--10 GHz range. It is important to note that there is a significant decrease in short uv baselines for the 11--12 GHz sub-band due to the high amount of RFI. A comparison of the uv sampling is shown in Figure \ref{fig:a478_uvsampling}. The implications of this unusually steep minihalo spectrum are discussed in the following chapter.
