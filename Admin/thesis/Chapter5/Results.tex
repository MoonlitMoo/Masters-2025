The following sections present background information and summaries of previous minihalo studies for each cluster. The results from this work are presented here, with their broader implications discussed in the following chapter.

\section{RXJ1720+2638}
\label{sec:rxj1720+2638}
RXJ1720+2638 (also known as RXJ1720.1+2638) is a cool core galaxy cluster ($M_{500}=5.9\times10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=1.39$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016})\footnote{This mass was calculated for a redshift of 0.164. The value of $R_{500}$ for this cluster, and the following ones, are calculated from the spherical mass overdensity relation $M_{\Delta} = \frac43\pi R_\Delta^3\Delta\rho(z)$ and matching cosmology with the mass.} at $z = 0.160$ \citep{Owers2011}. Early X-ray observations detected the presence of two cold fronts, a sharp edge to the southeast and an extended plateau to the northwest \citep{Mazzotta2001}. This suggested the presence of a dense, cool gas core ($T \approx 4$ keV) embedded within a more diffuse and hotter ICM ($T\approx10$ keV), with the cold fronts likely arising via a minor merger. Weak gravitational lensing studies \citep{Okabe2010}, together with galaxy density mapping \citep{Owers2011}, revealed the presence of a substructure approximately 550 kpc north of the cluster core. \citet{Owers2011} suggested that a northward passage of this substructure, passing east of the cluster core, provided the perturbation required to induce large-scale gas sloshing. RXJ1720+2638 was one of the first two clusters where cold fronts and minihalos were found to exhibit a spatial correlation, with the minihalo emission bounded by the cold fronts \citep{Mazotta2008}. 

A detailed spectral study was performed by \citet{Giacintucci2014a}, who analysed observations at six frequencies from 317 MHz to 8.4 GHz taken by the GMRT and VLA. To perform accurate comparisons between these frequencies, they limited all baseline ranges to 1--50 k$\lambda$, to enforce a unified sampling of the \textit{uv}-plane. They noted three primary radio components in the core region, the central radio galaxy (BCG, $\alpha = 0.87\pm0.03$), a head-tail radio galaxy to the northeast with the tail to the south ($\alpha = 1.05\pm0.04$, steepening down the tail), and diffuse minihalo emission. The BCG was noted to be unresolved with no jets or lobes detected at scales larger than 1.4 kpc. Higher angular resolution observations by the extended Multi-Element Radio Linked Interferometer Network (e-MERLIN) at 4.76 GHz found the BCG to exhibit sub-kpc jets \citep{Perrott2023}. These jets extend approximately 0.8 kpc to both the east and west, although though uncertainties in the recovered flux suggested an additional undetected extent. 

\citet{Giacintucci2014a} separated the minihalo emission into a central region with a diameter of 160 kpc and a tail extending approximately 200 kpc to the south. These were found to have spectral indices of $\alpha_c = 1.0\pm0.1$ and $\alpha_t = 1.5\pm0.1$, respectively. The core region exhibits a relatively flat spectral index across its extent, while clear spectral steepening is observed along the tail. Low-frequency LOFAR images at 144 MHz support the flat spectral index in the core, with pronounced steepening across the cold fronts \citep{Savini2019}. The spectral index for the core is consistent up to 4.86 GHz, however, the 8.44 GHz measurement was lower than predicted by the single power-law model. While this could indicate possible steepening, \citep{Giacintucci2014} note that the 4.86 GHz and 8.44 GHz observations suffered from significanly poorer \textit{uv} sampling, likely leading to flux losses through the filtering of large-scale emission. \citet{Perrott2021} performed a high-frequency measurement of the central minihalo region at 15.5 GHz, using the Arcminute Microkelvin Imager (AMI). By constructing a model of the minihalo directly in the \textit{uv}-plane to account for spatial filtering, they found that a single power-law with $\alpha = 1.0$ remains consistent up to 15.5 GHz. They similarly reprocessed the 8.44 GHz data and modelled in the \textit{uv}-plane, with the resulting best fitting model closely matching the single power-law spectrum. This demonstrated the previously observed steepening of \citet{Giacintucci2014} was caused by spatial filtering, rather than an intrinsic physical process. 

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
During imaging, two contaminating sources were identified prior to minihalo flux estimation (see Figure \ref{fig:rxj1720+2638_full_image}). These comprised the BCG (S1), embedded within the minihalo emission, and a variable AGN (S3) to the southwest. The head-tail source (S2) was well cleaned, with its tail emission isolated from that of the minihalo. Sources S1 and S3 were modelled individually per dataset with a \textit{uv}-cut of 12 k$\lambda$ and subsequently subtracted.

After subtraction, the minihalo emission was imaged using a \texttt{robust} of 1, to obtain a balance of beam sensitivity and PSF suppression. The multi-scale \texttt{tclean} scales were set to $[0, 4, 8, 16, 32]$ with a \texttt{smallscalebias} of $-0.5$. The wideband primary beam correction is applied to the subtracted image, correcting for loss of flux in the tail region. As \citet{Giacintucci2014a} provide details of the masking region, including the separation of centre and tail regions, the same masking was applied for flux estimation. A \textit{uv} constraint of 1--50 k$\lambda$ was also applied to ensure matched sampling. This allowed an accurate reproduction of these regions for analysis and comparison to previous studies.

The total minihalo flux density was measured as $8.61\pm0.56$ mJy, with the majority $7.60\pm0.52$ mJy attributed to the central region and only $1.01\pm0.35$ mJy arising from the tail. The measured flux densities for the minihalo and relevant sources are provided in Tables \ref{tab:mh_flux_densities} and \ref{tab:source_flux_densities}, respectively. The subtracted $\texttt{robust}=1$, primary beam corrected image is shown in Figure \ref{fig:rxj1720+2638_sub_image}. Spectral indices were fitted for the total minihalo emission, the central region, the tail, and the BCG using flux densities from this work combined with measurements from \citet{Giacintucci2014a} (excluding 8.46 GHz). The resulting spectral energy distributions are shown in Figure \ref{fig:rxj1720+2638_seds}. The spectral index for the total minihalo emission was $\alpha=1.1\pm0.06$, with a flatter central region at $\alpha_c = 1.08\pm0.06$, and a steep tail region of $1.41\pm0.09$.

Extrapolating the spectral index of the total minihalo emission found by \citet{Giacintucci2014a}, we find a predicted flux density of $S_p = 9.1\pm 1.4$ mJy, in agreement with the actual 10 GHz measurement. The extrapolation for the central and tail regions finds $S_{p, c} = 8.7\pm1.1$ mJy and $S_{p, t} = 0.34\pm0.13$, respectively. The central results agree within uncertainty with the previous measurements, while the tail is found to be three times brighter than expected. 

The BCG was measured to vary $\sim\!$20\% over one year (between C and D-configuration runs) and $\sim\!$10\% over one month (subsequent D-configurations). This degree of variation could suggest a systematic calibration error between the D-configuration observations. However, a systematic offset of only $0.7$\% was observed in the flux density models of the secondary calibrator for these observations. Other point sources in the image were also measured and no systematic offsets were observed. Given the lack of systematic offsets in other sources, together with the presence of active jets from the BCG, the observed variability is likely real.

\section{A478}

A478 is a nearby cool core galaxy cluster ($M_{500}=6.95\times10^{14}$, $R_{500}=1.31$ \citep{Planck2016}) at a redshift of $z = 0.088$ \citep{Liu2024}. \texttt{Chandra} and \textit{X-ray Multi-Mirror Mission} (XMM-Newton) observations reveal a relaxed cluster morphology with a slightly elliptical ICM structure and a central BCG hosting a double-lobed AGN \citep{Sun2003, Bourdin2008}. The core temperature is $T \approx 1.54$ keV, increasing to $T \approx 7.6$ keV at a radius of 260 kpc \citep{FanLam2024}. Two small ($\sim\!10$ kpc) X-ray cavities to the northeast and southwest are spatially aligned with the lobes of the BCG, indicating interaction between the BCG and the ICM \citep{Sun2003}. A cold front is detected approximately 60 kpc to the southwest of the cluster core \citep{Markevitch2003}. 

In the initial detection of minihalo emission, \citet{Giacintucci2014} identified several nearby radio sources to the minihalo. At 1.4 GHz, these include the central BCG (S1); a head-tail to the east with a tail to the northwest (S4); a second head-tail embedded in the tail of S4 and extending to the northeast (S2); and an unresolved source (S3) to the north of S2 and S4. The latter source, S3, is likely a background source due to the absence of an optical counterpart. \citet{Giacintucci2014} find the minihalo to be slightly more extended to the northeast ($R_{max} \sim 120$ kpc) compared to the other directions ($R_{min}\sim150$ kpc). This region blends with the tail of S4, resulting in a large uncertainty in the minihalo flux estimate due to difficulty in separating the tail emission from the diffuse minihalo. They find the minihalo emission to be largely confined by the southwest cold front, with a total flux density of $16.6\pm3$ mJy. LOFAR imaging at 144 MHz does not detect the diffuse minihalo emission, placing an upper limit on the spectral index of $\alpha < 1$, implying a flatter spectrum than most observed minihalos \citep{Savini2019}. Using archival GMRT data, \citet{Savini2019} find S1 and S4 to have a spectral indices of $\sim\!1.1$ and $\sim\!0.6$, respectively, between 144 and 610 MHz. 

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
The minihalo detected at 10 GHz is significantly smaller than observed at 1.4 GHz, measuring approximately 80 kpc north-south and 45 kpc east-west. This resulted in only two contaminating sources, S1 and S4 (see Figure \ref{fig:a478_image}). The S4 AGN doubled in brightness in the month between the D-configuration observations, causing severe PSF artefacts in the joint image. As such, this source was modelled on a per-dataset basis for the final self-calibration rounds. No comparable variation was observed in other compact sources, suggesting this behaviour is consistent with an AGN flare. The BCG exhibited variability consistent with the estimated calibration uncertainty over all observations. The sources S2 and S3 are detected, but outside the radius of the observed minihalo. Three faint compact sources are located between S2 and the minihalo, but are spatially separate from the minihalo emission at the $3\sigma$ level. Point subtraction was therefore limited to S1 and S4 (including the tail), using the standard 12 k$\lambda$ \textit{uv}-cut. This \textit{uv}-cut resulted in some residual extended emission from S4, however, it was spatially distinct and the artefacts were successfully suppressed. 

Following subtraction, the minihalo was modelled with scales $[0, 2, 4, 8, 16]$, \\\texttt{smallscalebias} of -0.5, and a \texttt{robust} of 1. The masking region was not explictly defined in \citet{Giacintucci2014}, so flux estimation mask was determined using the $3\sigma$ region as discussed in the previous chapter.

Combining this measurement with the 1.4 GHz measurement by \citet{Giacintucci2014}, the resulting spectral index is $\alpha=2.02\pm0.10$. This is exceptionally steep for a minihalo and is inconsistent with the spectral index upper limit reported by \citet{Savini2019}. To investigate further, the 8--12 GHz observation was split into four 1 GHz sub-bands. Each sub-band was imaged independently, and the total minihalo flux was computed using the spatial mask from the joint image. The resulting flux densities are listed in Table \ref{tab:a478_subbands}. Over the 8--12 GHz range the spectral index is even steeper at $\alpha=3.34\pm0.18$. The BCG spectral index is $0.78\pm0.02$, close to the $0.88\pm0.03$ measured over the full 1.4--10 GHz range. The sub-band fits and the total frequency fit are shown in Figure \ref{fig:a478_seds}. It is important to note that there is a significant decrease in short \textit{uv} baselines for the 11--12 GHz sub-band due to the high amount of RFI. A comparison of the \textit{uv} sampling is shown in Figure \ref{fig:a478_uvsampling}. The implications of this unusually steep minihalo spectrum are discussed in the following chapter.

\section{RXCJ1115+0129}
RXCJ1115+0129 (also known as MACS J1115.8+0129) is a large cool core cluster ($M_{500} = 6.86\times10^{14}$ \Msun, $R_{500} = 1.20$ Mpc\footnote{Derived using a flat cosmology with $H_0 = 70$ km s$^{-1}$ Mpc$^{-1}$, $\Omega_m=0.27$, $\Omega_\Lambda=0.73$.} \citep{Planck2016}) at $z=0.340$ \citep{Wen2015} that has recently been confirmed to host a minihalo. This cluster exhibits a close spatial alignment between the X-ray peak and the central BCG, and relatively regular elliptical morphology \citep{Ebeling2010, Pandey-Pommier2016}. \citet{Giovannini2020} reported a 7 mJy discrepancy between the flux density measured in the \textit{Faint Images of the Radio Sky at Twenty-Centimeters} (FIRST) survey\footnote{A 20 centimetre wavelength corresponds to a 1.4 GHz frequency.} and a deeper 1.4 GHz VLA observation. They suggest that the additional flux recovered in the VLA data, which is more sensitive to extended emission, corresponds to the brightest region of diffuse minihalo emission surrounding the central source. \citet{Trehaeven2023} subsequently confirmed the presence of the minihalo using MeerKAT archival data at 1.28 GHz. They find that the minihalo is orientated towards the northwest and southeast, aligned with the radio lobes of the central BCG. The minihalo extends 412 kpc along its longest axis, with an average diameter of 372 kpc. The minihalo flux density is $S_{1.28\> \text{GHz}}=7.91\pm2.59$ mJy, consistent with the discrepancy noted by \citet{Giovannini2020}. Using a 1--1.5 GHz sub-band comparison, they derive a spectral index of $\alpha=1.0 \pm 1.1$. The large uncertainty is due to the subtraction uncertainty of the strong BCG within the minihalo. They also measure a BCG flux density of $S_{1.28\>\text{GHz}}=8.73\pm0.88$ mJy and spectral index $\alpha=0.78\pm0.26$. They also note an unusual spatial variation in the spectral index across the minihalo, with significantly flatter values ($\alpha\sim0.5$) to the east and west, compared to steeper values ($\alpha\sim1.5$) toward the north and south.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
Initial examination of the 10 GHz image revealed four bright sources, including the BCG (S1). The remaining sources (S2-S4) are compact and located outside an 800 kpc radius of the central region (see the $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ Figure \ref{fig:rxcj1115_image}). The BCG increased in flux density by $\sim\!25\%$ between the C and D-configuration observations. As joint imaging was not significantly affected by this variation, no separate flux modelling was applied during self-calibration. In addition to S1, sources S2 and S3 were subtracted to suppress PSF artefacts resulting from their variability. After subtraction, the minihalo was imaged with $\texttt{robust}=1$, scales $[0, 4, 8]$, followed by primary beam correction. Masking for flux estimation was performed within the $3\sigma$ contour.

The total minihalo flux was measured as $0.97\pm0.15$ mJy. As with \citet{Trehaeven2023}, the uncertainty is dominated by the subtraction contribution $\sigma_{sub}=0.14$ mJy. The BCG has a measured flux density of $2.27\pm0.21$ mJy. Combining these measurements with those of \citet{Trehaeven2023}, the derived spectral index for the minihalo emission is $\alpha=1.03\pm0.18$, while the BCG spectral index is $0.66\pm0.07$. These findings are consistent with \citet{Trehaeven2023}, and offers a lower uncertainties by a factor of 6 over the broader frequency range. Figure \ref{fig:rxcj1115_sub_image} displays the $\texttt{robust}=1$  minihalo image, which reveals two mirrored decreases of flux in the region of S1. These features are likely due to imperfect source subtraction, however, their contribution is incorporated into the subtraction uncertainty.

The spatial extent of the minihalo is $\sim\!160$ kpc along the northwest-southeast axis, and $\sim\!100$ kpc on the perpendicular axis. This is orientated similarly to that found by \citet{Trehaeven2023}, though narrower in the perpendicular. This may reflect the steepening observed towards the north and south tapering the emission at these higher frequencies.

\section{RXJ2129+0005}
RXJ2129+0005 is a relaxed cool core cluster ($M_{500}=4.33\times10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=1.06$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016}) at redshift $z=0.234$ \citep{Sanders2011}. \textit{Chandra} X-ray observations measure a central core temperature of $T=4.5\pm0.5$ keV, rising to $T=7.4\pm1.2$ keV at a radius of $\sim\!200$ kpc \citep{Cavagnolo2009}. The X-ray emission is elongated along the northeast-southwest direction, with a cold front identified $\sim\!65$ kpc to the southwest \citep{Jimnez2024}. Weaker cold fronts are evidenced to the southeast and northwest, consistent with widespread gas sloshing in the core. 

The BCG is closely aligned with the cluster centre. GMRT observations at 235 and 610 MHz measure flux densities of $87\pm5$ mJy and $48.9\pm2.5$, respectively \citep{Kale2015}. VLA observations at 8.46 GHz reveal a compact core and a small ($\sim\!4$ kpc) jet extending southwards, with some evidence of much fainter counter-jet to the north \citep{Giacintucci2019}. Across this frequency range, the BCG has a spectral index of $\alpha=0.77\pm0.02$.

\citet{Kale2015} also reported the discovery of a minihalo surrounding the central BCG using the aforementioned GMRT observations at 235 and 610 MHz. The minihalo extends 225 kpc along the east-west axis and 110 kpc along the north-south axis. They measure total minihalo flux densities of $S_{235\>\text{MHz}} = 21\pm1.6$ mJy and $S_{610\>\text{MHz}} = 8\pm0.7$ mJy. An additional estimate at 1.4 GHz of $2.4$ mJy was obtained by subtracting the compact source flux measured by FIRST from that measured in the \textit{NRAO VLA Sky Survey} (NVSS). \citet{Giacintucci2019} detected the minihalo in a GMRT observation at 1.3 GHz, measuring the total emission as $2.4\pm0.2$ mJy, comparable with the previous estimate. They derived a spectral index of $\alpha=1.2\pm0.1$ for the minihalo between 235 MHz and 1.4 GHz.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
Self-calibration was successful for three of the four datasets, with phase solutions for one C-configuration observation failing to converge. The phase solutions were inconsistent across the frequency band, varying between -50\textdegree{} and 50\textdegree{}. This observation \\(ID: \texttt{24A-411.sb45228321.eb45252099.60350.80962270833}) also included two other science targets, A2626 and ACTJ0022-0036, which did not exhibit similar phase calibration failures.

Two bright sources are present in the central region: the BCG (S1) and a second radio galaxy to the south (S2). Source S2 is not explictly mentioned by \cite{Giacintucci2019}, however, negative residuals at its coordinates indicate that it was subtracted. The BCG varied by $\sim\!20$\% between the remaining C and D-configuration observations, producing radial PSF artefacts in the $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ image (Figure \ref{fig:rxj2129_image}). Only S1 was subtracted, as S2 was not modelled in the 12 k$\lambda$ \textit{uv}-cut image. Following subtraction, the minihalo was imaged with $\texttt{robust}=1$ and multiscales of $[0, 3, 8, 16, 32]$, before primary beam correction. The mask used for the calculation of the flux density was estimated based on that shown by \citep{Giacintucci2019} (see Figure 3a of paper). To account for the contribution of the unsubtracted source S2, the region within 5" was excluded from the mask. The resulting image and mask is shown in Figure \ref{fig:rxj2129_sub_image}.

The total minihalo and BCG flux densities were measured as $0.41\pm0.10$ mJy and $5.36\pm0.25$ mJy, respectively. The uncertainty in the measured minihalo flux was dominated by subtraction uncertainty, $\sigma_{sub} = 0.99$ mJy. Combining these measurements with previous results, the spectral indices of the minihalo and BCG were calculated as $\alpha=1.18\pm0.09$ and $\alpha=0.78\pm0.02$, respectively. The spectra are shown in Figure \ref{fig:rxj2129_seds}. Extrapolating from the previously measured minihalo spectral index yields an expected flux density of $0.21\pm0.05$. This is consistent within uncertainties with the measured value, although the observed flux is slightly higher than the extrapolated mean. The derived BCG spectral index is likewise consistent with the previous measurements.

\section{2A0335+096}
2A0335+096 is a nearby, low-mass cluster ($M_{500} = 2.27 \times 10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=0.92$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016}),\footnote{Retrieved as PSZ2 G176.27-35.04, which has a slight offset to the default coordinates of 2A0335+096. The value for R$_{500}$ was inferred from M$_{500}$.} at $z = 0.036$ \citep{Sanders2011}, the lowest redshift of this sample. \textit{Chandra} X-ray observations reveal a cool core with an average temperature of $T \leq 3.6$ keV within R$_{2500}$ \citep{Cavagnolo2009,Giacintucci2017}. Two X-ray cavities are detected to the northwest and southeast of the central BCG, alongside several bright X-ray structures within the central 20 kpc \citep{Mazzotta2003}. A cold front is observed approximately 40 kpc south of the cluster centre. These features are indicative of gas sloshing in the core, while the outer regions ($r > 40$ kpc) exhibit a symmetric, slightly elliptical X-ray surface brightness distribution \citep{Mazzotta2003}. 

Early VLA radio observations at 1.5 GHz discovered two fossil radio lobes around the BCG (S1) and a separate relic lobe located $\sim\!18$ kpc northwest of the core \citep{Sarazin1995}. 144 MHz LOFAR observations confirm that the fossil lobes are spatially aligned with the X-ray cavities \citep{Birzan2020, Ignesti2022}. The relic lobe is interpreted as aged plasma from an older AGN outburst \citep{Giacintucci2019}. The central BCG has a nearby ($\sim\!5$ kpc) compact source, connected with a tail of emission, that is identified as a companion galaxy (S2) \citep{Sarazin1995, Giacintucci2019}. The BCG exhibits two active lobes aligned northeast and southwest, rotated clockwise with respect to the fossil lobes. \citet{Giacintucci2019} measure the flux density of the BCG at 1.4 GHz as $S_{BCG, 1.4\> \text{GHz}} = 16\pm1$ mJy, and the companion galaxy as $S_{S2, 1.4\> \text{GHz}} = 1\pm0.1$ mJy. The fossil lobes were also measured: the southeast lobe has  a flux density $4.1\pm0.2$ mJy, the northwest lobe $2.7\pm0.1$ mJy, and the outer northwest lobe at $3.1\pm0.2$ mJy. \citet{Ignesti2022} detect the fossil lobes at 144 MHz and in 610 MHz GMRT observations, and report an estimated spectral index of $\alpha \approx 1.5\pm0.1$.

A head-tail galaxy is located $\sim\!330$ kpc to the northwest, with a long tail extending 1 Mpc to the south and passing to the west of the minihalo \citep{Ignesti2022}. As with minihalos, this spatial extent exceeds that expected from spatial diffusion over the CRe lifetime. \citet{Ignesti2022} suggest that interaction between the tail and the ICM may generate localised turbulence, which re-energises the tail CRe in a process termed `gentle reacceleration'.

\citep{Sarazin1995} also detected larger diffuse emission corresponding to the minihalo, which extends over 70 kpc. They measured a flux of $22.6$ mJy, although did not state an uncertainty. \citet{Giacintucci2019} subtracted the contributions from S1, S2, and the fossil lobes, to measure a total minihalo flux of $21.1\pm2.1$ mJy at 1.4 GHz. \citet{Ignesti2022} measure the minihalo emission at 144 MHz to be $387\pm53.9$ mJy, which they combined with the previous measurement to estimate a spectral index of $\alpha=1.2\pm0.1$. They also analysed a 610 MHz GMRT observation, but were unable to estimate the minihalo flux due to insufficient sensitivity of the data. As the minihalo exhibits a flatter spectrum than the fossil lobes, this implies it originates from comparatively younger or more energetic CRe. \citet{Ignesti2022} suggest that the CRe forming the minihalo are not injected from the lobes and subsequently diffused spatially, and must instead be reaccelerated. Their results support the hadronic and turbulent reacceleration scenarios, with no clear discriminating evidence.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
During self-calibration, several bright sources were located in the central region. These include the BCG, companion galaxy, and the two active lobes of the BCG. The self-calibration of the core was challenged by the presence of significantly negative model points ($\gtrsim-50$ mJy) in the vicinity of the BCG. Separate imaging of the individual datasets revealed that this issue was localised to the C-configuration data. Robust values were varied over $[-1, -0.5, 0.5, 1]$ in an attempt to minimise this occurrence. However, an fully satisfactory self-calibration was not achieved. The resulting image with $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ is shown in Figure \ref{fig:2a0335_image}. The point S3 may correspond to a peak of the southeast fossil lobe, though these are not clearly visible in the image. To further investigate the diffuse emission of the minihalo and fossil lobes, the active lobes, S1, and S2 were subtracted using models derived from a $12$ k$\lambda$ \textit{uv}-cut image.

Following this subtraction, the minihalo was re-imaged with $\texttt{robust}=1$ and multiscales of \\$[0, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64]$, followed by primary beam correction. The resulting image is displayed in Figure \ref{fig:2a0335_sub_image}. The diffuse emission of the southeast fossil lobe is difficult to disentangle from the minihalo emission in this region. The peak S3 is likely associated with this lobe, based on comparisons to the subtracted image of \citet{Giacintucci2019}. The northwest fossil lobe is not reliably detected at the $3\sigma$ level. The measured flux densities for the BCG and S2 are $3.01\pm0.18$ mJy and $0.27\pm0.07$ mJy, respectively. The shape of the BCG lobes is still observable in the subtracted image, evidencing that the subtraction is imperfect. Due to this, and the residual fossil lobe emission, no reliable measurement of the minihalo flux was performed. 

The expected fossil lobe flux can be estimated by extrapolating the 1.4 GHz flux values measured by \citet{Giacintucci2019} using the spectral index $\alpha \approx 1.5\pm0.1$ reported by \citet{Ignesti2022}. These fluxes are $0.21\pm0.04$ mJy for the southeast lobe, $0.14\pm0.03$ mJy for the northwest lobe, and $0.16\pm0.03$ mJy for the relic lobe. \citet{Giacintucci2019} approximate the radius of the fossil lobes as $\approx\! 17.5$ kpc and $\approx \!25$ kpc for the relic lobe. Using the 10 GHz restoring beam size of $6.7"\times5.1"$ and the estimated lobe radii, the total flux corresponds to a sum over approximately 17.4 synthesised beams, with an associated rms $\sigma_{rms}\sqrt{17.4}$. If we assume that the flux density within the fossil lobes is relatively uniform across each region, then the corresponding flux densities per beam are approximately $12.3\pm3.5$ $\mu$Jy beam$^{-1}$ for the southeast lobe, $8.1\pm2.0$ $\mu$Jy beam$^{-1}$ for the northwest lobe, and $9.3\pm2.2$ $\mu$Jy beam$^{-1}$ for the relic lobe. These rough estimates are comparable to the $3\sigma$ threshold of $7.68$ $\mu$Jy in the subtracted image, illustrating the difficulty in disentangling fossil lobe emission from the surrounding minihalo.

\section{A2204}
A2204 is a low-mass cool core cluster ($M_{500}=1.84\times10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=0.82$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016}) at redshift $z=0.152$ \citep{Sanders2011}. Detailed \textit{Chandra} X-ray observations reveal a complex core morphology, characterised by a prominent clockwise spiral pattern \citep{Sanders2005}. The central temperature is $T=3.7\pm0.2$ keV, rising to $T\approx8$ keV at a radius of 100 kpc. Within this region, two cold fronts are identified to the east ($\sim\!28$ kpc) and west ($\sim\!55$ kpc), delineating the spiral structure. This is similar to the simulations of \citet{ZuHone2013} and the morphology of RXJ1720+2638. Numerous $\sim\!4$ kpc depressions in the X-ray surface brightness of the inner core ($r\leq20$ kpc) may correspond to cavities. However, a lack of spatially aligned radio emission means that these cannot be confirmed as radio bubbles \citep{Sanders2009}. 

The central BCG consists of a compact nucleus (S1) and a companion galaxy (S2) located $\sim\!25$ kpc to the north \citep{Sanders2005, Sanders2009}. VLA observations at 8.4 and 1.4 GHz reveal weak components north and south of the BCG, potentially corresponding to AGN lobes. \citep{Giacintucci2014} reprocessed the 1.4 GHz observations and obtained flux densities of $58.9\pm2.9$ mJy and $1.6\pm0.1$ mJy for S1 and S2, respectively. After subtracting these components, they identified a minihalo with a radius of 50 kpc and a total flux density of $8.6\pm0.9$ mJy. The spatial extent matches that of the cold fronts, suggesting that the emission is confined by the sloshing fronts.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
Three sources were identified during self-calibration: the BCG (S1), its companion galaxy (S2), and a northwest compact source (S3). Imaging with $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ reveals a northward protrusion from the BCG, with no corresponding southern component at this frequency (Figure \ref{fig:a2204_image}). Source S2 is blended with the emission of S1 at the angular resolution of the D-configuration observations. To minimise contribution from S2 for flux estimation, a fitting radius of 4" was adopted for the Gaussian modelling. This resulted in $\sim\!20\%$ variability between the C and D-configuration measurements, likely reflecting intrinsic variability. Phase calibration was performed using a joint model to aid in disentangling the emission from S1 and S2 in the D-configuration datasets. Sources S1 and S2 were subtracted from each dataset using their respective sky models from a 12 k$\lambda$ \textit{uv}-cut image. 

Following this subtraction, the minihalo was imaged with $\texttt{robust}=1$ and multiscales $[0, 2, 4, 8, 16]$, before primary beam correction. The resulting image features significant negative radial residuals, particularly in the northeast-southwest axis, indicating imperfect subtraction of S1 (Figure \ref{fig:a2204_sub_image}). An attenuation of the minihalo emission is evident along the northwest-southeast axis, forming a `butterfly'-like pattern. Independent modelling of each subtracted dataset showed that these artefacts originate from the C-configuration data. Several attempts were made to improve the subtraction for this dataset by varying the modelling parameters of the \textit{uv}-cut image, however, no satisfactory solution was achieved. As these artefacts affect emission beyond the region of the subtraction, a reliable uncertainty estimate cannot be obtained, and therefore, no estimate of the minihalo flux is reported. 

The BCG flux was measured as $11.1\pm1.0$ mJy, which combined with the previous 1.4 GHz measurement, this yields a spectral index of $\alpha=0.85\pm0.05$. The flux of S2, from the resolved C-configuration data only, was $0.15\pm0.02$ mJy. Combined with the 1.4 GHz measurement, this provides a spectral index of $\alpha = 1.2\pm0.1$, indicative of a steep-spectrum radio galaxy.

\section{MS1455+2232}
\label{sec:ms1455+2232}
MS1455+2232 (also referred to as Z7160 and ZwCl 1454.8+2233) is a low-mass cluster ($M_{500}=3.5\times10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=1.02$ Mpc \citep{Giacintucci2017})\footnote{\citet{Giacintucci2017} derive this mass from the average cluster temperature $T_X$ (excluding the core) using the $M_{500}$--$T_X$ relation found by \citet{Vikhlinin2009}.} at redshift $0.258$ \citep{Wen2015}. The central temperature is $4\pm0.5$ keV, rising to $5.5\pm0.5$ keV at a radius of $\sim\!200$ kpc \citep{Mazzotta2001-2}. Two cold fronts have been identified, one $\sim\!140$ kpc to the north and the other $\sim\!40$ kpc to the south of the centre \citep{Mazotta2008}. These features form a clear spiral structure, indicative of gas sloshing \citep{Mazotta2008}. \citet{Riseley2022} performed a detailed analysis of this cluster using \textit{Chandra} X-ray, 144 MHz LOFAR, and 1300 MHz MeerKAT observations. They found that the sloshing spiral is slightly asymmetrical and elongated towards the southwest, covering a region $254\times232$ kpc. Given similarities to RXJ1720+2638 and numerical simulations, they suggest that this sloshing may be the result of a minor merger, though no studies have specifically searched for substructure in the galaxy distribution. 

The minihalo was first reported by \citet{Venturi2008} in 610 MHz GMRT observations, surrounding a central source. They measured the longest extent as 360 kpc, with a total flux density of $43.6\pm2.2$ mJy. VLA observations at 1.4 GHz only partially resolved the minihalo, with a maximum extent of $\sim\!230$ kpc and total flux density of $8.5\pm1.1$ mJy \citep{Giacintucci2019}. The sensitive LOFAR and MeerKAT imaging by \citet{Riseley2022} found a largest linear size of 586 kpc at 1300 MHz. They measured the total flux densities as $S_{145\> \text{MHz}} = 154.5\pm15.6$ mJy and $S_{1300\> \text{MHz}} = 18.7\pm0.9$ mJy, yielding a spectral index of the minihalo as $\alpha = 1.07\pm0.1$. 

The size of the minihalo notably exceeds the radius of the sloshing spiral core, implying that the emission is not contained by the cold fronts. \citet{Riseley2022} calculated the average spectral index within the spiral core and outside, finding that the differences were within $1\sigma$. They also noted the minihalo exhibits multiple radio components, with a sharp exponential taper in radio brightness to the north at a 100 kpc radius, while a second component appears to the south at the same radius, exhibiting a lower exponential decrease. These features were insufficient to determine whether hadronic production or turbulent reacceleration is favoured, with a combination of the two providing the best explanation. 

The BCG was investigated by \citep{Riseley2022}, who found a two-component power-law fit, with a break frequency of $3.1\pm1.4$ GHz. Below this frequency the spectrum was consistent with a relatively flat power law of $\alpha_{low} = 0.45\pm0.05$, while at higher frequencies it steepens to $\alpha_{high}=0.81\pm0.18$. There was a bright western component and much fainter eastern component associated with the BCG, of uncertain origin.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
Several bright sources were identified during self-calibration, including the central BCG (S1), an eastern head-tail (S2), and a southern compact source (S3). The latter source is a highly variable quasar\footnote{A quasar is an extremely luminous AGN.} that induced severe artefacts during self-calibration. This source was individually modelled for each dataset in an attempt to mitigate these issues, however, this was only partially successful. The joint $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ image is shown in Figure \ref{fig:ms1455_image}, depicting this issue. As these residuals directly interfere with the emission of S2, additional artefacts from this source are introduced. 

This becomes particularly evident following the subtraction of S1 and S2. Imperfect modelling and subtraction of S2 result in artefacts from S3 that contaminate the diffuse emission of the minihalo. An image of the minihalo produced using $\texttt{robust}=1$, scales $[0, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32]$, and $\texttt{smallscalebias}=-0.5$ is presented in Figure \ref{fig:ms1455_sub_image}. Negative and positive correlations blend with the central minihalo emission, preventing reliable separation of diffuse structure at the $3\sigma$ contour. Further work is required to achieve a satisfactory subtraction and allow an unbiased estimate of the minihalo flux density. The flux densities of the point sources are reported in Table \ref{tab:source_flux_densities}.

\section{A1413}
\label{sec:a1413}
A1413 is an non-cool core galaxy cluster ($M_{500}=5.95\times10^14$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=1.22$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016}) at redshift $z=0.139$ \citep{Wen2015}. It is currently the only known non-cool core cluster to host a minihalo. The dynamical state of this cluster was initially classified as undisturbed, due to an elongated and elliptical morphology \citep{Govoni2009,Giacintucci2017}. More recent \textit{Chandra} and XMM-Newton observations noted the absence of a sharp temperature decline in the core, which is uncharacteristic of a cool core system \cite{Botteon2018,Lusetti2024}. This borderline relaxed state has been interpreted as evidence for a weak cool core system, potentially stabilising after a past merger. The current consensus suggests A1413 exhibits neither a fully relaxed nor a highly disturbed morphology \citep{Riseley2023, Trehaeven2023, Lusetti2024}.

Diffuse minihalo emission was reported by \citet{Govoni2009} using 1.4 GHz VLA observations. They measured a total flux density of $1.9\pm0.7$ mJy over a spatial scale of $\sim\!220$ kpc. \citet{Savini2019} confirmed this detection with 144 MHz LOFAR observations and reported a size of $\sim\!210$ kpc, total flux density of $40\pm7$ mJy, and spectral index of $\alpha\approx1.3$. A similar spatial extent was found by \citet{Trehaeven2023} using 1.3 GHz MeerKAT observations, who found a total flux density of $2.05\pm0.27$ mJy. They also measured an in-band spectral index of $\alpha=1.5\pm0.46$.

Deeper observations with 144 MHz LOFAR and 1.3 GHz MeerKAT discovered a much larger halo $\sim\!500$ kpc \citep{Riseley2022}. The total flux densities at 144 MHz and 1.3 GHz over the larger extent were measured as $29.5\pm3.4$ mJy and $3.23\pm0.17$ mJy, respectively. The spectral index over the larger halo had a median value of $\langle\alpha\rangle=1.18\pm0.11$. Tentative evidence of steepening towards the edges of the minihalo was reported, with $\langle\alpha\rangle_{outer}=1.12\pm0.13$ compared to $\langle\alpha\rangle_{inner}=0.97\pm0.07$. 

Several sources were identified within the central minihalo, including the BCG, an eastern head-tail radio galaxy, and a third galaxy to the southwest. The spectral index of the BCG was measured as $\alpha = 1.13\pm0.07$ \citep{Riseley2023}. They suggested that this steep AGN spectrum was due to radio emission being dominated by lobes rather than the core.

\subsection{10 GHz observations}
The sources observed during self-calibration did not exhibit significant variability. After several rounds of self-calibration, the solutions did not fully converge, remaining within -10\textdegree{} and 10\textdegree{}. The prominent sources in the image are the central BCG (S3), its companion head-tail galaxy (S2), two nearby radio galaxies to the southwest (S1, S4), and a more distant head-tail galaxy to the west (S5). These are imaged using $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ and shown in Figure \ref{fig:a1413_image}. The minihalo is difficult to discern due to the prominence of the embedded sources. Sources S1--S4 were subtracted using the model from a 12 k$\lambda$ \textit{uv}-cut image. 

The subtracted datasets were imaged using $\texttt{robust}=1$ and multiscales $[0, 4, 8, 16, 32]$, followed by primary beam correction (Figure \ref{fig:a1413_sub_image}). Residual emission from S2 and S4 remains, complicating the estimation of minihalo flux. This is complicated by the large synthesised beam, likely due to the lack of remaining C-configuration data compared to the other clusters. Further work is required to robustly disentangle the complex source emission from that of the diffuse minihalo, in order to produce a minihalo flux estimation.

\section{ACTJ0022-0036}
ACTJ0022-0036 is a galaxy cluster ($M_{500}=5.5\times10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=0.92$ Mpc \citep{Hasselfield2013})\footnote{Derived using a Universal Pressure Profile and SZ measurements.} at redshift $z=0.805$ \citep{Menanteau2013}. It is the most distant cluster known to host a minihalo. Only limited information is available regarding the clusters dynamical state, as no dedicated studies have been conducted. \citet{Knowles2019} fitted temperature models to \textit{Chandra} X-ray data, with the best-fitting model having a temperature drop towards the core and a central temperature $T = 3.2^{+1.7}_{-0.7}$ keV. Although this is an indication of a cool core, this was only significant at the $2\sigma$ level, lending tentative evidence. 

Diffuse emission surrounding the BCG was reported by \citet{Knowles2019} using 610 MHz GMRT observations and subsequently categorised as a minihalo. To disentangle the diffuse component from the BCG emission, they fitted a two-component model to the radio surface brightness. This modelled the BCG as a 2D Gaussian source and the minihalo as a 2D exponential. Their best-fitting model revealed a strong preference for the minihalo component. They estimated the radius from the model as $\sim\!125$ kpc and integrated the model surface brightness within this radius to derive a total flux density of $6.4\pm1.8$ mJy. The corresponding model for the BCG resulted in a flux density of $46.9\pm0.2$ mJy at 610 MHz. This was combined with a FIRST measurement of $19.02\pm0.93$ mJy at 1.4 GHz, yielding the BCG spectral index as $\alpha = 0.9\pm0.1$.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
The self-calibration indicated four sources of minimal time-variability. These include the BCG (S1), a bright source to the east (S2), a compact source to the west (S3), and a bright source to the north (S4). The observations were imaged with $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ and shown in Figure \ref{fig:actj0022_image}. \citet{Knowles2019} identified S3 as a cluster member. S2 and S4 were not identified in their analysis, potentially due to their location beyond $R_{500}$. In both the 610 MHz GMRT and the 10 GHz observation, S4 exhibited mild extension. In the higher angular resolution VLA images, there is some evidence of a protrusion to the southeast and to the north from a compact core. To image the minihalo, S1 was individually subtracted from each dataset using the model from a 12 k$\lambda$ \textit{uv}-cut image. After subtraction, the datasets were imaged using $\texttt{robust}=1$ and scales $[0, 2, 4, 8, 16]$, followed by primary beam correction. The resulting image is shown in Figure \ref{fig:actj0022_sub_image}.

The subtracted image has clear evidence of over-subtraction of the AGN, with negative residuals at the centre. However, the $+3\sigma$ contours in the surrounding region are potential indicators of diffuse emission. With more careful modelling and subtraction of the BCG, the minihalo emission may be recovered at 10 GHz. The subtracted model likely contains the BCG and minihalo flux, with the measured BCG flux density in the image used to generate the model as $3.89\pm0.04$ mJy. Extrapolating from the prior estimate of the spectral index, the expected flux density for the BCG is $S_p = 2.25\pm0.37$ mJy. This is significantly lower than the measured value, providing further evidence for the contamination of minihalo emission in the BCG model.

\section{Z3146}
Z3146 (also known as ZwCl 1021+0426) is a cool core cluster ($M_{500}=6.53\times10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=1.2$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016}) at redshift $z=0.289$. \textit{Chandra} X-ray observations identified three cold fronts, two located $\sim\!13$ and $\sim\!35$ kpc north of the core, and a third front lying $\sim\!150$ kpc to the southeast \citep{Forman2002}. XMM-Newton observations report a core temperature of $\sim\!5.5$ keV, rising to $\sim\!7$ keV at a radius of $\sim\!150$ kpc \citep{Romero2020}. X-ray surface brightness fluctuations detected by XMM-Newton depict variations in the density and pressure, reflecting sloshing gas in the core \citep{Romero2023}.

VLA observations at 4.9 GHz and 8.5 GHz revealed a minihalo with a radius of 90 kpc \citep{Giacintucci2014}. Two point sources corresponding to the BCG and a background galaxy at $z=0.34$ are embedded in the diffuse emission. These have spectral indices of $\alpha=0.66\pm0.14$ and $\alpha=-0.33\pm0.16$, respectively, in this frequency range. After subtraction of these sources, \citet{Giacintucci2014} reported a minihalo flux density of $1.7\pm0.2$ mJy at 4.9 GHz. As the minihalo was not reliably detected at 8.5 GHz, the spectral indices of S1 and S2 were used to extrapolate flux densities at 1.4 GHz. These were then subtracted from a 1.4 GHz NVSS measurement to yield an estimated minihalo flux of $\sim\!5.2$ mJy. This corresponds to a spectral index $\alpha\approx1$ for the minihalo. \citet{Kale2015} image the minihalo using 610 MHz GMRT observations. They report a largest linear extent of 190 kpc, similar to that found at 4.9 GHz. They measured a total flux density of 11.5 mJy, with no stated uncertainty, which is consistent with $\alpha\approx1$ over 610 MHz to 4.9 GHz.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
Several prominent sources were identified during self-calibration. These include the BCG (S1), the background galaxy (S2), an eastern two-component system (S3, S4), a southern double-lobe galaxy (S5), and a second double-lobe source to the northeast (S6). The sources S3 and S4 are the brightest objects in the image and exhibit significant variability between the observations. These sources were modelled independently for each dataset to aid self-calibration. However, negative point fluxes were continually present in the models of these sources. This caused severe positive and negative sidelobes across the image. Several robusts between -1 and 1 were trialled alongside various scales, but a satisfactory clean model was not obtained. Figure \ref{fig:z3146_image} shows these observations imaged with $\texttt{robust}=1$ and scales of $[0, 2, 4]$. A sidelobe artefact passes directly across the minihalo emission visible around S1, contamination the flux values. As such, no estimate for the total minihalo flux can be derived. Further work is required to sufficiently model and subtract the contaminating effects from the minihalo emission.

\section{A1795}
A1795 is a cool core cluster ($M_{500}=4.47\times10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=1.15$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016}) at redshift $z=0.062$ \citep{Sanders2011}. \textit{Chandra} X-ray observations yield a central temperature of $3.36\pm0.12$ keV, rising to $6.49\pm0.43$ keV at a radius of $160$ kpc \citep{Ettori2002}. The morphology beyond the cool core is reported to be relaxed, with a slight elongation to the north. A cold front was also detected $\sim\!78$ kpc to the south, suggesting gas sloshing in the core \citep{Markevitch2001}. 

VLA observations at 1.4 GHz revealed a minihalo surrounding an extremely bright central BCG \citep{Giacintucci2014}. Arcsecond resolution imaging of the BCG exhibited two prominent lobes extending to the north and south, before bending to the east and west, respectively. The length of the lobes is $\sim\!10$ kpc. The flux density of the BCG was measured as $917\pm46$ mJy from a FIRST image at 1.4 GHz. A second source is located to the west of the BCG, and was associated with a background galaxy at $z=0.57$ \citep{Giacintucci2014}. The radius of the minihalo was estimated at $\sim\!100$ kpc, and the total flux density at 1.4 GHz was reported as $85\pm5$ mJy. \citet{Kale2015} attempted to image the minihalo using GMRT observations at 1.3 GHz. However, strong artefacts centred on the BCG prevented usable image quality and the cluster was excluded from further analysis.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
Self-calibration was complicated by the presence of the bent lobes of the BCG (S1). Modelling tended towards overestimating the lobe flux and subsequently compensating by inserting significant ($\geq50$ $\mu$Jy) negative point model. These resulted in severe sidelobe artefacts across the image. Small scales aided in modelling at high thresholds ($>20\sigma$). However, deeper clean thresholds($\leq20\sigma$) reintroduced the negative model points. As such, a self-calibrated image was not obtained. Two other prominent sources are present in the image, the aforementioned background galaxy S2, and a head-tail galaxy to the southeast. Flux values are not provided for these sources due to uncertainty in the self-calibration. A joint image using $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ and scales $[0, 2, 4]$ is shown in Figure \ref{fig:a1795_image} to illustrate these artefacts. Figure \ref{fig:a1795_zoom} depicts a closer view of the BCG, showing the lobe structure.

\section{A2626}
A2626 is a nearby low-mass galaxy cluster ($M_{500}=1.84\times10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=0.85$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016}) at redshift $z=0.056$ \citep{Wen2015}. XMM-Newton X-ray observations revealed temperature variations of only $\sim\!1$ keV out to a radius of 600 kpc \citep{Wong2008}. The central temperature was measured as $\sim\!2.5$ keV, rising to $\sim\!3.5$ keV at a radius of 230 kpc, before declining at larger radii. Two extended X-ray regions were identified $\sim\!450$ kpc to the northeast and southwest from the centre, attributed to smaller merging subclusters \citep{Mohr1996, Wong2008}. The cluster morphology is therefore complex, but not highly disturbed.

Several unusual arc-like radio sources have been detected in the core of A2626. Four arcs were observed in a symmetric diamond pattern around the central BCG, convex with respect to the centre \citep{Gitti2013,Ignesti2017,Kale2017}. The arcs were detected by VLA observations at 1.4 GHz, but not at 5 GHz, implying a steep spectrum \citep{Gitti2013}. GMRT observations at 610 MHz confirmed a steep spectrum with a reported spectral indices in the range 3.2--3.6 \citep{Kale2017}. They also measured the linear arc extents in the range 79--152 kpc. Within the region defined by the arcs, \citet{Gitti2004,Gitti2013} report diffuse emission proposed to be a minihalo with an extent of 60 kpc.  \citet{Ignesti2017} measured the spectral index of the inner diffuse emission separately from the arcs using VLA observations at 3 and 5 GHz  They found a relatively constant average spectral index of $\alpha=0.7\pm0.1$ in this region, significantly different from the arcs. This minihalo candidate remains to be confirmed as the smaller scale may allow it to be a result of AGN activity.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
Self-calibration was unable to cleanly model the central BCG. Significant negative point models were introduced at even high thresholds ($\geq\sigma20$). These introduced radial sidelobe artefacts across the full image. Robusts were trialled between -2 and 1, alongside various scales, without success. A self-calibrated image was not obtained for this cluster. An image using $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ and scales $[0, 2, 4, 8]$ is presented in Figure \ref{fig:a2626_image}. The central BCG (S1) is visible, alongside a double-lobe compact source (S2) to the west. The lobes protrude north and south for $\sim\!10$ kpc and slightly bend towards the west. No flux densities are estimated for these sources due to uncertainty in image quality.
