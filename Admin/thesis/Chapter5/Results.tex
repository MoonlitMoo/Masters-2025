The following sections present background information and summaries of previous minihalo studies for each cluster. The results from this work are presented here, with their broader implications discussed in the following chapter.

\section{RXJ1720+2638}
\label{sec:rxj1720+2638}
RXJ1720+2638 (also known as RXJ1720.1+2638) is a cool core galaxy cluster ($M_{500}=5.9\times10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=1.39$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016})\footnote{This mass was calculated for a redshift of 0.164. The value of $R_{500}$ for this cluster, and the following ones, are calculated from the spherical mass overdensity relation $M_{\Delta} = \frac43\pi R_\Delta^3\Delta\rho(z)$ and matching cosmology with the mass.} at $z = 0.160$ \citep{Owers2011}. Early X-ray observations detected the presence of two cold fronts, a sharp edge to the southeast and an extended plateau to the northwest \citep{Mazzotta2001}. This suggested the presence of a dense, cool gas core ($T \approx 4$ keV) embedded within a more diffuse and hotter ICM ($T\approx10$ keV), with the cold fronts likely arising via a minor merger. Weak gravitational lensing studies \citep{Okabe2010}, together with galaxy density mapping \citep{Owers2011}, revealed the presence of a substructure approximately 550 kpc north of the cluster core. \citet{Owers2011} suggested that a northward passage of this substructure, passing east of the cluster core, provided the perturbation required to induce large-scale gas sloshing. RXJ1720+2638 was one of the first two clusters where cold fronts and minihalos were found to exhibit a spatial correlation, with the minihalo emission bounded by the cold fronts \citep{Mazotta2008}. 

A detailed spectral study was performed by \citet{Giacintucci2014a}, who analysed observations at six frequencies from 317 MHz to 8.4 GHz taken by the GMRT and VLA. To perform accurate comparisons between these frequencies, they limited all baseline ranges to 1--50 k$\lambda$, to enforce a unified sampling of the uv plane. They noted three primary radio components in the core region, the central radio galaxy (BCG, $\alpha = 0.87\pm0.03$), a head-tail radio galaxy to the northeast with the tail to the south ($\alpha = 1.05\pm0.04$, steepening down the tail), and diffuse minihalo emission. The BCG was noted to be unresolved with no jets or lobes detected at scales larger than 1.4 kpc. Higher angular resolution observations by the extended Multi-Element Radio Linked Interferometer Network (e-MERLIN) at 4.76 GHz found the BCG to exhibit sub-kpc jets \citep{Perrott2023}. These jets extend approximately 0.8 kpc to both the east and west, although though uncertainties in the recovered flux suggested an additional undetected extent. 

\citet{Giacintucci2014a} separated the minihalo emission into a central region with a diameter of 160 kpc and a tail extending approximately 200 kpc to the south. These were found to have spectral indices of $\alpha_c = 1.0\pm0.1$ and $\alpha_t = 1.5\pm0.1$, respectively. The core region exhibits a relatively flat spectral index across its extent, while clear spectral steepening is observed along the tail. Low-frequency LOFAR images at 144 MHz support the flat spectral index in the core, with pronounced steepening across the cold fronts \citep{Savini2019}. The spectral index for the core is consistent up to 4.86 GHz, however, the 8.44 GHz measurement was lower than predicted by the single power-law model. While this could indicate possible steepening, \citep{Giacintucci2014} note that the 4.86 GHz and 8.44 GHz observations suffered from significanly poorer uv sampling, likely leading to flux losses through the filtering of large-scale emission. \citet{Perrott2021} performed a high-frequency measurement of the central minihalo region at 15.5 GHz, using the Arcminute Microkelvin Imager (AMI). By constructing a model of the minihalo directly in the uv plane to account for spatial filtering, they found that a single power-law with $\alpha = 1.0$ remains consistent up to 15.5 GHz. They similarly reprocessed the 8.44 GHz data and modelled in the uv plane, with the resulting best fitting model closely matching the single power-law spectrum. This demonstrated the previously observed steepening of \citet{Giacintucci2014} was caused by spatial filtering, rather than an intrinsic physical process. 

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
During imaging, I identified two contaminating sources prior to minihalo flux estimation (see Figure \ref{fig:rxj1720+2638_full_image}). These comprised the BCG (S1), embedded within the minihalo emission, and a variable AGN (S3) to the southwest. The head-tail source (S2) was well cleaned, with its tail emission isolated from that of the minihalo. Sources S1 and S3 were modelled individually per dataset with a uv cut of 12 k$\lambda$ and subsequently subtracted.

After subtraction, the minihalo emission was imaged using a \texttt{robust} of 1, to obtain a balance of beam sensitivity and PSF suppression. The multi-scale \texttt{tclean} scales were set to $[0, 4, 8, 16, 32]$ with a \texttt{smallscalebias} of $-0.5$. The wideband primary beam correction is applied to the subtracted image, correcting for loss of flux in the tail region. As \citet{Giacintucci2014a} provide details of the masking region, including the separation of centre and tail regions, I applied the same masking for flux estimation. A uv constraint of 1--50 k$\lambda$ was also applied to ensure matched sampling. This allowed an accurate reproduction of these regions for analysis and comparison to previous studies.

The total minihalo flux density was measured as $8.61\pm0.56$ mJy, with the majority $7.60\pm0.52$ mJy attributed to the central region and only $1.01\pm0.35$ mJy arising from the tail. The measured flux densities for the minihalo and relevant sources are provided in Tables \ref{tab:mh_flux_densities} and \ref{tab:source_flux_densities}, respectively. The subtracted $\texttt{robust}=1$, primary beam corrected image is shown in Figure \ref{fig:rxj1720+2638_sub_image}. Spectral indices were fitted for the total minihalo emission, the central region, the tail, and the BCG using flux densities from this work combined with measurements from \citet{Giacintucci2014a} (excluding 8.46 GHz). The resulting spectral energy distributions are shown in Figure \ref{fig:rxj1720+2638_seds}. The spectral index for the total minihalo emission was $\alpha=1.1\pm0.06$, with a flatter central region at $\alpha_c = 1.08\pm0.06$, and a steep tail region of $1.41\pm0.09$.

Extrapolating the spectral index of the total minihalo emission found by \citet{Giacintucci2014a}, we find a predicted flux density of $S_p = 9.1\pm 1.4$ mJy, in agreement with the actual 10 GHz measurement. The extrapolation for the central and tail regions finds $S_{p, c} = 8.7\pm1.1$ mJy and $S_{p, t} = 0.34\pm0.13$, respectively. The central results agree within uncertainty with the previous measurements, while the tail is found to be three times brighter than expected. 

The BCG was measured to vary $\sim\!$20\% over one year (between C and D-configuration runs) and $\sim\!$10\% over one month (subsequent D-configurations). This degree of variation could suggest a systematic calibration error between the D-configuration observations. However, a systematic offset of only $0.7$\% was observed in the flux density models of the secondary calibrator for these observations. Other point sources in the image were also measured and no systematic offsets were observed. Given the lack of systematic offsets in other sources, together with the presence of active jets from the BCG, the observed variability is likely real.

\section{A478}

A478 is a nearby cool core galaxy cluster ($M_{500}=6.95\times10^{14}$, $R_{500}=1.31$ \citep{Planck2016}) at a redshift of $z = 0.088$ \citep{Liu2024}. Chandra observations reveal a relaxed cluster morphology with a slightly elliptical ICM structure and a central BCG hosting a double-lobed AGN \citep{Sun2003, Bourdin2008}. The core temperature is $T \approx 1.54$ keV, increasing to $T \approx 7.6$ keV at a radius of 260 kpc \citep{FanLam2024}. Two small ($\sim\!10$ kpc) X-ray cavities to the northeast and southwest are spatially aligned with the lobes of the BCG, indicating interaction between the BCG and the ICM \citep{Sun2003}. A cold front is detected approximately 60 kpc to the southwest of the cluster core \citep{Markevitch2003}. 

In the initial detection of minihalo emission, \citet{Giacintucci2014} identified several nearby radio sources to the minihalo. At 1.4 GHz, these include the central BCG (S1); a head-tail to the east with a tail to the northwest (S4); a second head-tail embedded in the tail of S4 and extending to the northeast (S2); and an unresolved source (S3) to the north of S2 and S4. The latter source, S3, is likely a background source due to the absence of an optical counterpart. \citet{Giacintucci2014} find the minihalo to be slightly more extended to the northeast ($R_{max} \sim 120$ kpc) compared to the other directions ($R_{min}\sim150$ kpc). This region blends with the tail of S4, resulting in a large uncertainty in the minihalo flux estimate due to difficulty in separating the tail emission from the diffuse minihalo. They find the minihalo emission to be largely confined by the southwest cold front, with a total flux density of $16.6\pm3$ mJy. LOFAR imaging at 144 MHz does not detect the diffuse minihalo emission, placing an upper limit on the spectral index of $\alpha < 1$, implying a flatter spectrum than most observed minihalos \citep{Savini2019}. Using archival GMRT data, \citet{Savini2019} find S1 and S4 to have a spectral indices of $\sim\!1.1$ and $\sim\!0.6$, respectively, between 144 and 610 MHz. 

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
The minihalo detected at 10 GHz is significantly smaller than observed at 1.4 GHz, measuring approximately 80 kpc north-south and 45 kpc east-west. This resulted in only two contaminating sources, S1 and S4 (see Figure \ref{fig:a478_image}). The S4 AGN doubled in brightness in the month between the D-configuration observations, causing severe PSF artefacts in the joint image. As such, this source was modelled on a per-dataset basis for the final self-calibration rounds. No comparable variation was observed in other compact sources, suggesting this behaviour is consistent with an AGN flare. The BCG exhibited variability consistent with the estimated calibration uncertainty over all observations. The sources S2 and S3 are detected, but outside the radius of the observed minihalo. Three faint compact sources are located between S2 and the minihalo, but are spatially separate from the minihalo emission at the $3\sigma$ level. Point subtraction was therefore limited to S1 and S4 (including the tail), using the standard 12 k$\lambda$ uv cut. This uv cut resulted in some residual extended emission from S4, however, it was spatially distinct and the artefacts were successfully suppressed. 

Following subtraction, the minihalo was modelled with scales $[0, 2, 4, 8, 16]$, \\\texttt{smallscalebias} of -0.5, and a \texttt{robust} of 1. The masking region was not explictly defined in \citet{Giacintucci2014}, so flux estimation mask was determined using the $3\sigma$ region as discussed in the previous chapter.

The total minihalo flux was measured as $0.31 \pm 0.03$ mJy. Associated compact sources are listed in Table \ref{tab:source_flux_densities}. Combining this measurement with the 1.4 GHz measurement by \citet{Giacintucci2014}, the resulting spectral index is $\alpha=2.02\pm0.10$. This is exceptionally steep for a minihalo and is inconsistent with the spectral index upper limit reported by \citet{Savini2019}. To investigate further, the 8--12 GHz observation was split into four 1 GHz sub-bands. Each sub-band was imaged independently, and the total minihalo flux was computed using the spatial mask from the joint image. The resulting flux densities are listed in Table \ref{tab:a478_subbands}. Over the 8--12 GHz range the spectral index is even steeper at $\alpha=3.34\pm0.18$. The BCG spectral index is $0.78\pm0.02$, close to the $0.88\pm0.03$ measured over the full 1.4--10 GHz range. The sub-band fits and the total frequency fit are shown in Figure \ref{fig:a478_seds}. It is important to note that there is a significant decrease in short uv baselines for the 11--12 GHz sub-band due to the high amount of RFI. A comparison of the uv sampling is shown in Figure \ref{fig:a478_uvsampling}. The implications of this unusually steep minihalo spectrum are discussed in the following chapter.

\section{RXCJ1115+0129}
RXCJ1115+0129 (also known as MACS J1115.8+0129) is a large cool core cluster ($M_{500} = 1.07\times10^{15}$ \Msun, $R_{500} = 1.39$ Mpc\footnote{Derived using a flat cosmology with $H_0 = 70$ km s$^{-1}$ Mpc$^{-1}$, $\Omega_m=0.27$, $\Omega_\Lambda=0.73$.} \citep{Umetsu2016}) at $z=0.350$ \citep{Planck2016} that has recently been confirmed to host a minihalo. This cluster exhibits a close spatial alignment between the X-ray peak and the central BCG, and relatively regular elliptical morphology \citep{Ebeling2010, Pandey-Pommier2016}. \citet{Giovannini2020} reported a 7 mJy discrepancy between the flux density measured in the \textit{Faint Images of the Radio Sky at Twenty-Centimeters} (FIRST) survey\footnote{A 20 centimetre wavelength corresponds to a 1.4 GHz frequency.} and a deeper 1.4 GHz VLA observation. They suggest that the additional flux recovered in the VLA data, which is more sensitive to extended emission, corresponds to the brightest region of diffuse minihalo emission surrounding the central source. \citet{Trehaeven2023} subsequently confirmed the presence of the minihalo using MeerKAT archival data at 1.28 GHz. They find that the minihalo is orientated towards the northwest and southeast, aligned with the radio lobes of the central BCG. The minihalo extends 412 kpc along its longest axis, with an average diameter of 372 kpc. The minihalo flux density is $S_{1.28\> \text{GHz}}=7.91\pm2.59$ mJy, consistent with the discrepancy noted by \citet{Giovannini2020}. Using a 1--1.5 GHz sub-band comparison, they derive a spectral index of $\alpha=1.0 \pm 1.1$. The large uncertainty is due to the subtraction uncertainty of the strong BCG within the minihalo. They also measure a BCG flux density of $S_{1.28\>\text{GHz}}=8.73\pm0.88$ mJy and spectral index $\alpha=0.78\pm0.26$. They also note an unusual spatial variation in the spectral index across the minihalo, with significantly flatter values ($\alpha\sim0.5$) to the east and west, compared to steeper values ($\alpha\sim1.5$) toward the north and south.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
Initial examination of the 10 GHz image revealed four bright sources, including the BCG (S1). The remaining sources (S2-S4) are compact and located outside an 800 kpc radius of the central region (see the $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ Figure \ref{fig:rxcj1115_image}). The BCG increased in flux density by $\sim\!25\%$ between the C and D-configuration observations. As joint imaging was not significantly affected by this variation, no separate flux modelling was applied during self-calibration. In addition to S1, sources S2 and S3 were subtracted to suppress PSF artefacts resulting from their variability. After subtraction, the minihalo was imaged with $\texttt{robust}=1$, scales $[0, 4, 8]$, followed by primary beam correction. Masking for flux estimation was performed within the $3\sigma$ contour.

The total minihalo flux was measured as $0.97\pm0.15$ mJy. As with \citet{Trehaeven2023}, the uncertainty is dominated by the subtraction contribution $\sigma_{sub}=0.14$ mJy. The BCG has a measured flux density of $2.27\pm0.21$ mJy. Combining these measurements with those of \citet{Trehaeven2023}, the derived spectral index for the minihalo emission is $\alpha=1.03\pm0.18$, while the BCG spectral index is $0.66\pm0.07$. These findings are consistent with \citet{Trehaeven2023}, and offers a lower uncertainties by a factor of 6 over the broader frequency range. Figure \ref{fig:rxcj1115_sub_image} displays the $\texttt{robust}=1$  minihalo image, which reveals two mirrored decreases of flux in the region of S1. These features are likely due to imperfect source subtraction, however, their contribution is incorporated into the subtraction uncertainty.

The spatial extent of the minihalo is $\sim\!160$ kpc along the northwest-southeast axis, and $\sim\!100$ kpc on the perpendicular axis. This is orientated similarly to that found by \citet{Trehaeven2023}, though narrower in the perpendicular. This may reflect the steepening observed towards the north and south tapering the emission at these higher frequencies.

\section{RXJ2129+0005}
RXJ2129+0005 is a relaxed cool core cluster ($M_{500}=4.33\times10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=1.06$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016}) at redshift $z=0.234$ \citep{Sanders2011}. \textit{Chandra} X-ray observations measure a central core temperature of $T=4.5\pm0.5$ keV, rising to $T=7.4\pm1.2$ keV at a radius of $\sim\!200$ kpc \citep{Cavagnolo2009}. The X-ray emission is elongated along the northeast-southwest direction, with a cold front identified $\sim\!65$ kpc to the southwest \citep{Jimnez2024}. Weaker cold fronts are evidenced to the southeast and northwest, consistent with widespread gas sloshing in the core. 

The BCG is closely aligned with the cluster centre. GMRT observations at 235 and 610 MHz measure flux densities of $87\pm5$ mJy and $48.9\pm2.5$, respectively \citep{Kale2015}. VLA observations at 8.46 GHz reveal a compact core and a small ($\sim\!4$ kpc) jet extending southwards, with some evidence of much fainter counter-jet to the north \citep{Giacintucci2019}. Across this frequency range, the BCG has a spectral index of $\alpha=0.77\pm0.02$.

\citet{Kale2015} also reported the discovery of a minihalo surrounding the central BCG using the aforementioned GMRT observations at 235 and 610 MHz. The minihalo extends 225 kpc along the east-west axis and 110 kpc along the north-south axis. They measure total minihalo flux densities of $S_{235\>\text{MHz}} = 21\pm1.6$ mJy and $S_{610\>\text{MHz}} = 8\pm0.7$ mJy. An additional estimate at 1.4 GHz of $2.4$ mJy was obtained by subtracting the compact source flux measured by FIRST from that measured in the \textit{NRAO VLA Sky Survey} (NVSS). \citet{Giacintucci2019} detected the minihalo in a GMRT observation at 1.3 GHz, measuring the total emission as $2.4\pm0.2$ mJy, comparable with the previous estimate. They derived a spectral index of $\alpha=1.2\pm0.1$ for the minihalo between 235 MHz and 1.4 GHz.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
Self-calibration was successful for three of the four datasets, with phase solutions for one C-configuration observation failing to converge. The phase solutions were inconsistent across the frequency band, varying between -50\textdegree{} and 50\textdegree{}. This observation \\(ID: \texttt{24A-411.sb45228321.eb45252099.60350.80962270833}) also included two other science targets, A2626 and ACTJ0022-0036, which did not exhibit similar phase calibration failures.

Two bright sources are present in the central region: the BCG (S1) and a second radio galaxy to the south (S2). Source S2 is not explictly mentioned by \cite{Giacintucci2019}, however, negative residuals at its coordinates indicate that it was subtracted. The BCG varied by $\sim\!20$\% between the remaining C and D-configuration observations, producing radial PSF artefacts in the $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ image (Figure \ref{fig:rxj2129_image}). Only S1 was subtracted, as S2 was not modelled in the 12 k$\lambda$ uv cut image. Following subtraction, the minihalo was imaged with $\texttt{robust}=1$ and multiscales of $[0, 3, 8, 16, 32]$, before primary beam correction. The resulting image is shown in Figure \ref{fig:rxj2129_sub_image}.

The spectra are shown in Figure \ref{fig:rxj2129_seds}. Extrapolating from the previously measured minihalo spectral index yields an expected flux density of $0.21\pm0.05$. This is consistent within uncertainties with the measured value, although the observed flux is slightly higher than the extrapolated mean. The derived BCG spectral index is likewise consistent with the previous measurements.

\section{2A0335+096}
2A0335+096 is a nearby, low-mass cluster ($M_{500} = 2.27 \times 10^{14}$ \Msun{}, $R_{500}=0.92$ Mpc \citep{Planck2016}),\footnote{Retrieved as PSZ2 G176.27-35.04, which has a slight offset to the default coordinates of 2A0335+096. The value for R$_{500}$ was inferred from M$_{500}$.} at $z = 0.036$ \citep{Sanders2011}, the lowest redshift of this sample. \textit{Chandra} X-ray observations reveal a cool core with an average temperature of $T \leq 3.6$ keV within R$_{2500}$ \citep{Cavagnolo2009,Giacintucci2017}. Two X-ray cavities are detected to the northwest and southeast of the central BCG, alongside several bright X-ray structures within the central 20 kpc \citep{Mazzotta2003}. A cold front is observed approximately 40 kpc south of the cluster centre. These features are indicative of gas sloshing in the core, while the outer regions ($r > 40$ kpc) exhibit a symmetric, slightly elliptical X-ray surface brightness distribution \citep{Mazzotta2003}. 

Early VLA radio observations at 1.5 GHz discovered two fossil radio lobes around the BCG (S1) and a separate relic lobe located $\sim\!18$ kpc northwest of the core \citep{Sarazin1995}. 144 MHz LOFAR observations confirm that the fossil lobes are spatially aligned with the X-ray cavities \citep{Birzan2020, Ignesti2022}. The relic lobe is interpreted as aged plasma from an older AGN outburst \citep{Giacintucci2019}. The central BCG has a nearby ($\sim\!5$ kpc) compact source, connected with a tail of emission, that is identified as a companion galaxy (S2) \citep{Sarazin1995, Giacintucci2019}. The BCG exhibits two active lobes aligned northeast and southwest, rotated clockwise with respect to the fossil lobes. \citet{Giacintucci2019} measure the flux density of the BCG at 1.4 GHz as $S_{BCG, 1.4\> \text{GHz}} = 16\pm1$ mJy, and the companion galaxy as $S_{S2, 1.4\> \text{GHz}} = 1\pm0.1$ mJy. The fossil lobes were also measured: the southeast lobe has  a flux density $4.1\pm0.2$ mJy, the northwest lobe $2.7\pm0.1$ mJy, and the outer northwest lobe at $3.1\pm0.2$ mJy. \citet{Ignesti2022} detect the fossil lobes at 144 MHz and in 610 MHz GMRT observations, and report an estimated spectral index of $\alpha \approx 1.5\pm0.1$.

A head-tail galaxy is located $\sim\!330$ kpc to the northwest, with a long tail extending 1 Mpc to the south and passing to the west of the minihalo \citep{Ignesti2022}. As with minihalos, this spatial extent exceeds that expected from spatial diffusion over the CRe lifetime. \citet{Ignesti2022} suggest that interaction between the tail and the ICM may generate localised turbulence, which re-energises the tail CRe in a process termed `gentle reacceleration'.

\citep{Sarazin1995} also detected larger diffuse emission corresponding to the minihalo, which extends over 70 kpc. They measured a flux of $22.6$ mJy, although did not state an uncertainty. \citet{Giacintucci2019} subtracted the contributions from S1, S2, and the fossil lobes, to measure a total minihalo flux of $21.1\pm2.1$ mJy at 1.4 GHz. \citet{Ignesti2022} measure the minihalo emission at 144 MHz to be $387\pm53.9$ mJy, which they combined with the previous measurement to estimate a spectral index of $\alpha=1.2\pm0.1$. They also analysed a 610 MHz GMRT observation, but were unable to estimate the minihalo flux due to insufficient sensitivity of the data. As the minihalo exhibits a flatter spectrum than the fossil lobes, this implies it originates from comparatively younger or more energetic CRe. \citet{Ignesti2022} suggest that the CRe forming the minihalo are not injected from the lobes and subsequently diffused spatially, and must instead be reaccelerated. Their results support the hadronic and turbulent reacceleration scenarios, with no clear discriminating evidence.

\subsection{10 GHz observation}
During self-calibration, several bright sources were located in the central region. These include the BCG, companion galaxy, and the two active lobes of the BCG. The self-calibration of the core was challenged by the presence of significantly negative model points ($\gtrsim-50$ mJy) in the vicinity of the BCG. Separate imaging of the individual datasets revealed that this issue was localised to the C-configuration data. Robust values were varied over $[-1, -0.5, 0.5, 1]$ in an attempt to minimise this occurrence. However, an fully satisfactory self-calibration was not achieved. The resulting image with $\texttt{robust}=0.5$ is shown in Figure \ref{fig:2a0335_image}. The point S3 may correspond to a peak of the southeast fossil lobe, though these are not clearly visible in the image. To further investigate the diffuse emission of the minihalo and fossil lobes, the active lobes, S1, and S2 were subtracted using models derived from a $12$ k$\lambda$ uv cut image.

Following this subtraction, the minihalo was re-imaged with $\texttt{robust}=1$ and multiscales of \\$[0, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64]$, followed by primary beam correction. The resulting image is displayed in Figure \ref{fig:2a0335_sub_image}. The diffuse emission of the southeast fossil lobe is difficult to disentangle from the minihalo emission in this region. The peak S3 is likely associated with this lobe, based on comparisons to the subtracted image of \citet{Giacintucci2019}. The northwest fossil lobe is not reliably detected at the $3\sigma$ level. The measured flux densities for the BCG and S2 are $3.01\pm0.18$ mJy and $0.27\pm0.07$ mJy, respectively. The shape of the BCG lobes is still observable in the subtracted image, evidencing that the subtraction is imperfect. Due to this, and the residual fossil lobe emission, no reliable measurement of the minihalo flux was performed. 

The expected fossil lobe flux can be estimated by extrapolating the 1.4 GHz flux values measured by \citet{Giacintucci2019} using the spectral index $\alpha \approx 1.5\pm0.1$ reported by \citet{Ignesti2022}. These fluxes are $0.21\pm0.04$ mJy for the southeast lobe, $0.14\pm0.03$ mJy for the northwest lobe, and $0.16\pm0.03$ mJy for the relic lobe. \citet{Giacintucci2019} approximate the radius of the fossil lobes as $\approx\! 17.5$ kpc and $\approx \!25$ kpc for the relic lobe. Using the 10 GHz restoring beam size of $6.7"\times5.1"$ and the estimated lobe radii, the total flux corresponds to a sum over approximately 17.4 synthesised beams, with an associated rms $\sigma_{rms}\sqrt{17.4}$. If we assume that the flux density within the fossil lobes is relatively uniform across each region, then the corresponding flux densities per beam are approximately $12.3\pm3.5$ $\mu$Jy beam$^{-1}$ for the southeast lobe, $8.1\pm2.0$ $\mu$Jy beam$^{-1}$ for the northwest lobe, and $9.3\pm2.2$ $\mu$Jy beam$^{-1}$ for the relic lobe. These rough estimates are comparable to the $3\sigma$ threshold of $7.68$ $\mu$Jy in the subtracted image, illustrating the difficulty in disentangling fossil lobe emission from the surrounding minihalo.
