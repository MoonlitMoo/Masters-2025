# Diffuse Radio Emission

## Synchrotron Emission

Standard assumption for the ICM CR (cosmic ray, high energy electron/small particles) population is 

$$
n(E)dE \propto E^{-p}dE
$$

Where the energy (or momentum) index $p$ is related to the spectral index $\alpha$ by $p = 1 - 2\alpha$. Remember that the spectral index is the power law between the flux density and the frequency $F_\nu \propto \nu^\alpha$. Spectral index can then be calculated as $\alpha(\nu) = \frac{\partial \log F_\nu(\nu)}{\partial \log \nu}$. 

Diffuse cluster radio emission typically has a steep spectral index with $\alpha \lesssim - 1$. The shape is related to the acceleration mechanism and the energy losses. This then gives rise to an expected lifetime, which is $t_{age} \lesssim 10^8 \> \text{yrs}$ for clusters. Typically this would result in distances of 10 pc and well less than 1 Mpc, which means that larger diffuse sources cannot be from a single location. Therefore they must be re-accelerated or produced in these areas.

## Particle Acceleration Mechanisms

- First order Fermi Acceleration
Also knowns as diffuse shock acceleration (DSA), where particles are accelerated by passing back and forth over a shock front. These particles pass over the shock front as they are scattered by magnetic inhomogeneities. Causes a power-law energy distribution
- Second order Fermi acceleration
A stochastic process where the scattering itself causes losses or gains in energy. Random motion gives increase of energy slightly higher probability. Inefficient process.
- Adiabatic compression
Shock waves can compress a region of relativistic radio plasma from an AGN. This energy lossless compression means that CR electrons regain energy.
- Secondary models
This is where CR electrons are produced as byproducts. Hadronic model has relativistic protons and thermal ions colliding to produce these.

## Diffuse cluster radio sources

- Radio halo
Centrally located diffuse sources in merging clusters without optical components. These are not localised to a single source.
- Mini-halo
Smaller size than the majority of the ICM and in found in relaxed cool core clusters which usually have brightest cluster galaxy (BCG).
- Radio relics
Leftovers from previous events and exist on the periphery of clusters.

## Magnetic fields within clusters

Exist throughout the cluster, unknown origins, potentially combination of historic/primordial fields. There exist some relations between the central (thermal) electron density $n_{th}$ and mean central magnetic field $B$ strength as $B \propto n_{th}^\eta$, however, the large structure profile is largely unknown. We can estimate the strength as within an order of magnitude for various scenarios and the spatial scales (5 - 500 kpc) and coherence length (order 10 pc). 

## IC Emission

CR electrons should scatter CMB photons, causing a power-law of X-ray emissions alongside the bremsstrahlung. It’s difficult to separate this out and measure it because of how accurate your model needs to be. As such, no conclusive evidence of this. Would be good as also allows for estimation of the magnetic field.

# Radio Halos

## Giant Radio Halos

The distribution of the extended source typically follows the brightness distribution of the ICM. Typical sizes of 1-2 Mpc. Lot of uncertainty in the flux density measurements due to map noise, flux scale uncertainty, subtraction of discrete flux sources, incomplete uv coverage, calibration errors. 

Typical integrated spectral indices give $\alpha \in [-1.4, -1.1]$. This is based on measurements at only two frequencies, so large spectral baselines are rare. 

Theoretical models predict that the central fractional polarisation should be in the range 15%-30%, but we are unable to detect this due to the low sensitivity and resolution of existing interferometers. More knowledge would provide information on the magnetic field structure. 

Hadronic production ruled out as a source (for the Coma cluster) from observations. This is due to gamma ray observations which are expected from hadronic production.

## Mini-halos

With sizes around 100-500 kpc, they are found in cool-core relaxed clusters, with the emission surrounding the central loud BCG. Size is comparable to the central cool region. Radio emission cannot be from the central AGN due to the short lifetimes, and must also be created in-situ. 

It can be hard to separate emissions from the AGN lobes, mini-halo, and regions near the AGN that do not require re-acceleration. The central galaxy may be very bright, making detection of the mini-halo difficult at all.

Mini-halos tend to be confined by the cold fronts of cool core clusters. In this scenario the breaking of this could cause a mini halo to develop into a giant halo, or vice versa. There is also the case that these two objects could coexist. Substructures exist within a mini-halo, which may be related to variation in the ICM magnetic field strength, also suggesting mini-halos may not be fully diffuse. 

Spectral indices of mini halos tend to be similar to giant radio halos. Difficult to say since few detailed studies.

There isn’t a clear correlation between the radio power and cluster mass for these (there is for giant radio halos). They appear in 80% of cool core clusters, 0% non-cool core. Small evidence that low mass clusters are less likely to produce a mini halo.

The origin is a bit more constrained than the giant halos, with re-acceleration of turbulence not due to galaxy mergers, but instead gas sloshing within the cluster core. The source of electrons is also likely to be the central AGN. If MHD turbulence is causing re-acceleration, then there should be a spectral break from an energy distribution cutoff. Limited studies mean no clear conclusion. 

Gamma ray studies have not been able to rule out hadronic production.
